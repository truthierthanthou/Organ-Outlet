About the project

Organ Outlet is a platform for finding kidney sellers or “donors” all around the world, while highlighting the commodification of living people into body parts. It uses a satirical tone to make a heavy subject more accessible to the interactor while using current website and shopping conventions to convey a sense of irony and reflexivity about our current consumer practices. While the site itself is meant to be humorous, the information conveyed in the site is based on aggregating data from books and journalist reports on the illegal global organ trade.

Context

According to the Declaration of Istanbul, transplant commercialism refers to the exchange of money for the procurement of organs between a recipient or donor, while transplant tourism occurs when people travel outside their country to procure an organ. This practice requires a complex network of brokers, international patients, willing or not-so-willing sellers and medical professionals. Brokers are central to linking the different subjects, recruiting participants and organizing travel services for recipients that include accommodations, medical testing and bribes for 100,000 to 200,000 dollars.

Globally, at least 200,000 people are on waiting lists for kidneys. In 2012, 114,690 organs were reported to be transplanted. This only satisfies 10% of the global need. Of these organs, around 10,000 of them are illegally sold globally each year according to the group Organs Watch.

Inevitably the economically desperate and the vulnerable are the ones who end up selling their organs - the poor, people recovering from disasters, victims of war and political oppression. In a 2000 essay, Anthropologist Nancy Scheper-Hughes summarizes the supply chain of organs as coming "from South to North, from Third to First World, from poor to rich, from black and brown to white and from female to male". Fifteen years later, these unequal circumstances still persist.

Reasons for Participating

  • Dialysis is expensive, risky and taxing
  • Rising levels of diabetes and heart diseases
  • Long transplant lists
  • Legal restrictions per country
  • Cultural narratives ("saving my family", "sacrifice")

Risks to Donors

  • Not enough information about long-term effects (e.g., poor health, inability to perform physically demanding jobs)
  • Lack of post-operation medical care
  • Social ostracism
  • Decreased physical and mental well-being
  • Coercion, trickery and manipulation (e.g., getting cheated out of their pay)
  • Money spent very quickly; low return-on-investment

Risks to Recipients

  • Donors themselves forge their identities so they can participate in the system, leading to medical risks for the receiver
  • Medical complications for receivers (e.g., HIV, hepatitis B and C viruses)
  • Expensive anti-rejection drugs that lower immune system

What's next?

While there are many debates over the ethical issues of organ trade - as to whether regulation or banning is the solution - the demand for organs for scientific and medical reasons will not go away. While we seek ways on how to provide greater equity for the donors and receivers, altruistic organ donation - such as signing up for organ donation - must also be promoted as a viable alternative. One person's survival need not be at the expense of another person's lifetime of suffering.
