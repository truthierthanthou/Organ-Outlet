About the project

Organ Outlet is a critical data visualization platform about the underground kidney market.

Informed by ethnographic research and journalistic reports, it conveys the prices, players, and networks of the kidney trade, through a satirical, “user-friendly” e-commerce website. By doing so, it aims to reflect on broader issues of commodification of people into body parts, global inequality, technocapitalism, and inadequate healthcare.

Ultimately, it poses the question: whose lives are truly benefitting from this system?

This project was originally created for the course Principles of Interaction Design at Georgia Tech in 2015, under the guidance of Dr. Janet Murray.

What informed this work?

Transplant commercialism refers to the exchange of money for the procurement of organs between a recipient or donor, while transplant tourism occurs when people travel outside their country to procure an organ.

These practices require a complex network of brokers, international patients, willing or not-so-willing sellers, and medical professionals.

Brokers are central to linking the different subjects, recruiting participants and organizing travel services for recipients that include accommodations, medical testing and bribes for 100,000 to 200,000 dollars.

Why is this happening?

The demand for organs continue to rise because of increasing levels of diabetes and heart disease. In addition, dialysis is expensive, risky and taxing. The waiting lists are incredibly long. Legal & cultural restrictions for organ donation also vary per country.

Globally, at least 200,000 people are on waiting lists for kidneys. In 2012, 114,690 organs were reported to be transplanted. This only satisfies 10% of the global need. Of these organs, around 10,000 of them are illegally sold globally each year according to the group Organs Watch.

Inevitably, the economically desperate and the vulnerable are the ones end up selling their organs - the poor, people recovering from disasters, victims of war and political oppression.

In a 2000 essay, Anthropologist Nancy Scheper-Hughes summarizes the supply chain of organs as coming "from South to North, from Third to First World, from poor to rich, from black and brown to white and from female to male". Over a decade later, these unequal circumstances still persist.

What are the risks for participants?

Donors

  • Not enough information about long-term effects (e.g., poor health, inability to perform physically demanding jobs)
  • Lack of post-operation medical care
  • Social ostracism
  • Decreased physical and mental well-being
  • Coercion, trickery and manipulation (e.g., getting cheated out of their pay)
  • Money spent very quickly
  • Low return-on-investment

Receivers

  • Donors themselves forge their identities so they can participate in the system, leading to medical risks for the receiver
  • Medical complications for receivers (e.g., HIV, hepatitis B and C viruses)
  • Expensive anti-rejection drugs that lower immune system

What's next?

While there are many debates over the ethical issues of organ trade - as to whether regulation or banning is the solution - the demand for organs for scientific and medical reasons will not go away. The problems are systemic, and many people will continue to suffer, while others disproportionately profit.

While we seek ways on how to provide greater equity for the donors and receivers, altruistic organ donation - such as signing up for organ donation - must also be promoted as a viable alternative. One person's survival need not be at the expense of another person's lifetime of suffering.
